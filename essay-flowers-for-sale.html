Introduction

Every poison has a dose that kills and a dose that heals. In the imperial, China-esque world of The Apothecary Diaries (薬屋のひとりごと, Kusuriya no Hitorigoto), beauty, obedience, and knowledge function according to this same logic. Women in this world are cultivated like flowers: trained, displayed, evaluated, and discarded once damaged or depleted. They are grown for use rather than longevity; prized for appearance rather than endurance. Servants are trained to feign ignorance, but Maomao (猫猫, マオマオ), an apothecary turned servant, breaks that rule. She watches, calculates, and reads what others overlook: the shimmer of lead beneath powder and the dangers blooming quietly under silk. She knows that survival depends not on blooming brightest, but on knowing precisely when not to bloom at all.

The Apothecary Diaries took root on the Japanese web novel self-publishing website Shōsetsuka ni Narō (小説家になろう, Let's become a novelist) in 2011 by Hyūga Natsu (日向夏). In the following year, it caught the attention of Shufunotomo (主婦の友社, Shufu no Tomosha), a distinguished Japanese publisher, who acquired the rights to the story and published it as a single volume in 2012, and then, two years later, extended it into a light novel series, written by Hyūga Natsu and illustrated by Touko Shino (しのとうこ). At the end of 2025, the series has published 16 volumes and sold over 14 million copies, placing it among the best-selling historical fiction series of recent years. The series expanded into two independently ongoing manga adaptations in 2017, and now exceeds 22 million manga copies sold. A spin-off manga centered around Maomao’s friend, Xiaolan (小蘭, シャオラン), written by Natsu Hyūga and illustrated by Itsuki Nanao (七緒 一綺), also began publication at the beginning of 2025. Anime adaptation followed in 2023 and quickly became one of the highest-rated series of the year. Streaming data from Japanese and international platforms consistently places it in weekly top ranks. The second season premiered in 2024, solidifying the franchise’s popularity across demographics. The third season and an original story anime film are set to debut in the latter half of 2026.

These combined successes manifest the appeal and popularity of the story and its characters, especially the main protagonist, Maomao. Online discourse on how Maomao breaks female protagonist stereotypes in modern popular media has been circulating across many online platforms, featuring video essays on YouTube, discussion posts on Reddit, fan arts posted on Instagram, and memes shared on Twitter. The plot receives high praise as well. Set in a fictional empire inspired by late-imperial China—primarily relying on drawing its inspiration from the Tang dynasty (618–907) and incorporating scholarly knowledge from the late Ming dynasty (1368–1644)—the narrative blends court intrigue, medical investigation, and character-driven mystery, where Maomao uses her pharmacological training and pleasure district upbringing to navigate the plot’s central conflicts.

Maomao grows up knowing early on that beauty attracts danger, knowledge invites punishment, and the wrong concentrations of honesty can ruin a life faster than poison. When she is kidnapped and sold into service at the imperial palace in episode 1, she initially seeks invisibility, intending to endure her term quietly. However, that strategy collapses when Maomao, retaining her curious and eccentric personality, deduces that the emperor’s newborn children are being poisoned by leaded face powder used by their mothers. Her anonymous intervention saves their lives but exposes her literacy and medical competence, drawing her into the palace’s inner workings. When an oral dismissal order deliberately contradicts the written order to stay, meant only for the literate, Maomao hesitates, exposing herself as the sole servant who can read. Crucially, Maomao does not seize this visibility as empowerment. She attempts to erase her role, understanding that knowledge wielded too openly becomes a liability. As Hyūga Natsu has explained in interviews, the series is intentionally written with ambiguity and restraint, allowing knowledge to surface only in partial, controlled forms so that it remains ethical rather than destructive.1 This philosophy structures both Maomao’s actions and the narrative itself.

Throughout the anime’s opening sequences Maomao is repeatedly associated with wood sorrel, katabami (片喰), a low-growing oxalis whose small blossoms are easy to overlook. Unlike ornamental flowers cultivated for display, katabami thrives in shade rather than direct sunlight, spreading quietly along the ground. It is also a plant of dosage: used in folk medicine and cuisine yet associated with sourness and irritation when consumed in excess.2 Its value lies in its endurance and careful calibration. The symbolism aligns closely with Maomao’s own mode of survival. Like wood sorrel, she stays low, avoids unnecessary attention, and remains useful rather than decorative. The connection is reinforced linguistically as well. Katabami is known in some regional contexts as neko-ashi (ねこあし, “cat’s paw”), a nickname that resonates strongly with Maomao’s name and self-presentation.3 This repeated visual pairing of Maomao with katabami invites viewers to read her as a character who survives by inhabiting shadow rather than spectacle.4

By centering the story on Maomao’s calibrated perspective, The Apothecary Diaries reframes agency as a practice of dosage rather than defiance, and, by extension, captures the fragile forms of agency available to women within the imperial Sino-Japanese hierarchies. The story transforms the language of beauty, flowers [and herbs], into one of endurance, revealing how women navigate systems that would rather display them than hear. This paper examines how Maomao’s survival strategies—shaped by the pleasure district, refined within the palace, and rooted in historical women’s knowledge—redefine female strength as endurance, judgment, and selective visibility. In doing so, the series does more than present an unconventional heroine: it reclaims historical modes of feminine survival that have long been dismissed as passive, minor, or insignificant, reminding the audience that some forms of power are meant to grow in shade. Through Maomao, the series recovers the lives and knowledge of women who endured within male-dominated structures by remaining partially unseen. As a character, Maomao does not simply navigate these systems but preserves and reactivates the histories of women whose stories unfolded in the margins and rarely entered official records. In this sense, she functions as a reclamation figure, carrying forward forms of agency that literature and history have often allowed to wither.

1Toxic Systems: The Hanamachi and the Palace

In both the pleasure district (hanamachi) and the imperial rear palace, bodies are commodified, performances are evaluated, and emotions are diluted. Women’s values are determined by appearance, charm, and their ability to maintain social harmony. Knowledge, especially medical or toxicological, is treated as a dangerous weapon in a woman’s hands. The brothel district and the royal harem operate as two wings of the same stage that regulate femininity in different registers. The word hanamachi, which stands for the pleasure quarter (also known as red-light district, prostitution/courtesan quarter), literally means the “flower town” (花街)5 in Japanese, reinforcing the idea of women being cultivated as ornamental blooms meant for display. The imperial palace may drape itself in the most expensive silks and the most elaborate etiquette, but it follows the same fundamental logic: women are sacred objects of beauty and also disposable assets.

In the “flower town” (hanamachi), femininity is not a personal attribute but a trained performance. Beauty functions as currency and exposure: the more visible a woman becomes, the more fully she is made available for purchase and harm. The hanamachi, therefore, operates by dosage: courtesans learn how much charm to offer, how much emotion to simulate, and how much of themselves to reveal to survive without being consumed.6 The flower metaphor naturalizes this economy. Here, girls enter a brothel house as kamuro (禿, attendants) and learn by observing and serving older courtesans. Their training covers dance, music, poetry, posture, witty conversation, and everything that is meant to attract and entertain their elite male clientele. Courtesans are appraised by the rokuyoku (六欲), the “six attractions”: color (complexion and beauty), form (figure and bearing), carriage (grace in movement), voice (speech and song), softness (tenderness of demeanor), and features (facial beauty).7 This mirrors the refinements of aristocratic literati culture, but in a distorted form: where noblemen might cultivate poetry or calligraphy to gain respect, courtesans cultivate allure, charm, and skilled performances to survive. The result is a constant evaluation system. Young girls are sorted and ranked as either promising “beauty material” and later re-assessed at the moment of peak bloom, or relegated to lower roles, a brutal arithmetic that a child of hanamachi, like Maomao, witnesses from childhood. They observe as bodies become commodities, and fate is determined by looks and grace. Calling the pleasure district a “flower town” frames cultivation as culture and coercion as refinement.

The hanamachi also shapes Maomao’s relationship to knowledge. In a space that expects women to be decorative rather than competent, medical expertise is treated as destabilizing. Marran’s analysis of the “poison woman” figure helps clarify this fear. Historically, women associated with bodily knowledge—particularly toxins—are framed as seductive, deviant, or dangerous, their expertise recoded as a moral threat.8 Maomao inherits similar knowledge of plants, poisons, and dosage, but she refuses the narrative that would make her spectacular. She treats knowledge as responsibility rather than power. This early education forms the foundation for Maomao’s later survival in the palace. Before imperial authority ever attempted to regulate her, the hanamachi had already taught her the rules. The flower town exists to cultivate women for sale, and Maomao learned how to remain unsold.

Yet the hanamachi is not rendered as a site of pure victimhood. Within its constraints, women develop lateral forms of agency through artistry, reputation, and mutual dependence. Widmer’s study of late‑imperial courtesan writers shows how performance could become a platform for cultural authority, allowing some women to speak, circulate texts, and cultivate influence within systems designed to commodify them.9 The Apothecary Diaries preserves this tension. The pleasure district disciplines women harshly, but it also teaches skills—observation, emotional regulation, strategic self‑presentation—that can be redeployed elsewhere.

The imperial palace refines that same logic of commodification under the language of virtue, harmony, and order. When Maomao is kidnapped and sold into servitude in the imperial palace, she finds that this gilded world of emperors and concubines simply is the hanamachi in another dress. Like courtesans, the emperor’s consorts are ranked and evaluated relentlessly. As Bossler’s work on gender and court culture in late imperial China demonstrates, women’s value in palace systems was deeply tied to their capacity to produce heirs while remaining ideologically pure and controllable.10 A concubine who falls out of favor might as well wither like a cut flower. Much as the harmony in the pleasure district, the harmony in the rear palace depends on women’s exquisite obedience, charm, and submission. Just as in the pleasure quarter, the palace is infested with poisoning, jealousy, and competition among women who are priced against each other. The stakes are life and death, whether by literal poison or by the social death of disgrace. The imperial court uses more ornate language and etiquette to mask this brutal logic, magnified by imperial power.

The poisoned infants case exposes the lethal consequences of this refinement. The court’s insistence on idealized beauty—specifically the use of leaded white face powder—transforms aesthetic discipline into biological harm. Maomao identifies the cause through attention to bodily symptoms and material practice. Her diagnosis reveals a central contradiction: a system that prizes appearance above all else ultimately poisons its own future. In this moment, beauty ceases to be symbolic and becomes directly destructive. Crucially, Maomao attempts to intervene anonymously. She writes a warning rather than confronting authority, calibrating truth to minimize retaliation. This decision reflects the lessons learned in the hanamachi: knowledge wielded too visibly invites punishment. The palace expects women of her rank to be interchangeable and silent, and Maomao’s competence disrupts that expectation.

The palace’s medical hierarchy further reinforces this boundary. Formal medicine belongs to male eunuch physicians sanctioned by the court, while women’s medical knowledge is tolerated only when domestic, invisible, or derivative.11 This is a world where formal expertise is a male prerogative. A woman who knows too much is threatening. Maomao’s expertise occupies an unstable position: she possesses scientific skill without institutional legitimacy. She, therefore, decides it is safest to stay silent about the full extent of her knowledge. This narrative resonates with historical and even modern patterns. Contemporary scholarship on gender bias in Japanese medical institutions underscores the persistence of similar hierarchies, where women’s competence is often treated as suspect when it challenges established authority.12 Although separated by centuries, the logic remains familiar.

Through Maomao’s navigation of the palace, The Apothecary Diaries reveals how systems that claim refinement often intensify exploitation. The palace does not civilize the flower economy, it perfects it. Maomao’s outsider eyes strip away the decorations, revealing the palace for what it is: another cage of silk and gold, where women are both prizes and prisoners. She reads the system clearly and survives by regulating her own visibility. Like wood sorrel transplanted into harsher soil, she adapts without changing her nature, enduring by remaining partially unseen.

The pleasure district has become a familiar visual shorthand in contemporary anime and manga, often deployed as an exotic backdrop that signals danger, decadence, or forbidden desire. In these narratives, the “flower town” appears as a place of heightened drama without much emphasis on the women who inhabit it. A useful contrast is Demon Slayer: Kimetsu no Yaiba (鬼滅の刃), whose Entertainment District arc presents a sanitized and highly stylized version of the pleasure quarter, due to censorship and genre conventions. The courtesans and settings serve largely as an exotic backdrop for a battle against demons. One high-ranking courtesan, oiran (花魁), named Daki (堕姫), is revealed to be a demon. Her backstory hints at the tragedy of a young girl exploited and transformed into a monster, but this poignant thread is quickly overshadowed by the demands of shōnen (少年, genres for young boy) for action spectacle. In essence, one might argue that Demon Slayer uses the “flower town” primarily for drama and spectacle, suggesting female commodification, but not directly examining the lives of the women within it.

The Apothecary Diaries rejects this mode of representation. Instead of treating the hanamachi as aesthetic scenery, it centers on emotional realism and focuses on women’s experiences. This distinction is closely tied to authorship and genre. Written by a female author and situated within a josei (女性, genres for adult women) mystery framework, The Apothecary Diaries presents the hanamachi with a fidelity to both beauty and pain. The series does not shy away from the physical and emotional toll of the pleasure quarter, such as meticulous routines, strict hierarchies, the bodily risks (STDs, unwanted pregnancies, violence), and the subtle forms of trauma. Yet it also presents moments of grace and solidarity, like when courtesans share secret hopes or dance to honor one another. Unlike stories that flatten courtesans into tragic beauties or femme fatales, The Apothecary Diaries insists on their complexity. The courtesans here are artists, victims, survivors, and friends, whose elegance and talents are appreciated and the injustices they endure are recognized. Japanese feminist media critics have identified this kind of narrative restraint as a recurring strategy through which female characters exercise power without triggering the backlash often attached to overt rebellion.13 This nuanced portrayal of the hanamachi is rare in popular media, making The Apothecary Diaries stand out as a thoughtful reimagining of a familiar trope.

2Writing Women

Maomao’s story draws on literary traditions of how women are written and how they write themselves. Being a modern Japanese work set in a fictionalized historical China, The Apothecary Diaries blends narrative conventions from both cultures. The gendered literary traditions from classical Chinese court narratives and Japanese women’s diary literature are both important to understand the cultural footprint. The Apothecary Diaries combines multiple narrative forms: diary-like introspective, monogatari-style (物語), court intrigue and detective fiction.

Maomao’s narrative function becomes clearer when The Apothecary Diaries is read not only as a story about women but as a story written through modes historically associated with women’s writing. Across East Asian literary history, women’s texts have often emerged from marginal positions within elite, male-dominated cultures. Rather than producing grand historical chronicles or heroic epics, these writings record life as it is lived: through fragments, observations, lists, and routines. Scholarship on Heian-period (794–1185) women’s diaries and memoirs has emphasized that these texts do not lack authority, practice it differently. Edith Sarra argues that court women’s writing constructs narrative power through selective disclosure, positioning restraint and indirection as deliberate strategies rather than signs of passivity.14 Women write from thresholds, between public and private, where observation itself becomes a form of control.

The Apothecary Diaries can be seen as a modern heir to the women’s diary tradition. This light novel series shares its genealogy with the nikki (日記, diary) in its focus on daily life, keen observation, and personal voice. Maomao’s perspective is essentially that of a diary-writer: she notices the small details like the smell of a poison, the minor changes in a person’s complexion, and servants’ gossip. In fact, the Japanese title of the series, Kusuriya no Hitorigoto (薬屋のひとりごと), literally means “The Apothecary’s Soliloquy”. The word hitorigoto (can also be written as 独り言 or 一人言)15 implies speaking to oneself, which is parallel to the act of a diary writer recording private thoughts. The influence of Japanese court women’s memoirs can be seen in how Maomao’s inner voice is constructed: she is frank about bodily realities (like menstruation, injury, or the messiness of childbirth) and documents emotional undertones without open judgment, similar to how Heian women diarists would matter-of-factly note their joys and sorrows life.

The Apothecary Diaries captivates a broad audience with its unique blend of genres and narrative styles. The story operates through a hybrid structure that blends court intrigue, detective logic, and character-driven observation. At its core, the series is a mystery story where Maomao investigates puzzling intrigues, murders, and conspiracies in episodic arcs, using logical deduction and scientific knowledge. This places it in the lineage of Sherlockian tales, transposed to an East Asian palace setting with a female lead. Each “case” has clues laid out for the audience to piece together, granting the detective fiction satisfaction. However, unlike a pure detective series, the mysteries here are entangled with court politics and daily life events, which brings in the flavor of historical court drama. The large cast of political figures, consorts, and servants, and the ongoing intrigue of imperial succession and factional rivalry, act as the story’s backdrop. This hybridity is a mechanism through which feminine-coded knowledge is elevated from background detail to narrative machine.

Mystery as a genre depends on attention to what others overlook: inconsistencies, traces, routines, and minor deviations. In The Apothecary Diaries, these skills align almost perfectly with forms of knowledge historically assigned to women: cosmetics, household management, medicinal care, gossip, and bodily observations. Rather than treating these domains as trivial or ornamental, the series positions them as analytically powerful. Each case is resolved not through force or authority, but through attentiveness shaped by lived experience. This structure allows Maomao’s observational authority to function without requiring her to perform heroism in conventional terms. Detective logic supplies narrative momentum, but it is Maomao’s intimate familiarity with bodies, substances, and social hierarchies that makes resolution possible.

In many traditional tales, a woman with deep knowledge of poisons would be cast in a sinister light, such as the earlier discussed archetype of the dokufu (毒婦) or “poison woman” who transgresses feminine norms, often depicted as a seductive villain or a criminal. Indeed, Japanese literature of the Meiji period treated learned, scheming women as objects of fear, reflecting societal anxieties about female autonomy. Christine Marran traces this archetype in modern Japanese culture, showing how women linked to toxins are repeatedly framed as threats to social order rather than as skilled practitioners. Poison knowledge becomes evidence of moral corruption, its practical dimensions overshadowed by narratives of danger and transgression.16 The poison woman is not feared because she kills, but because she knows how bodies fail and because that knowledge resists containment. Moreover, in Japanese, the word doku can read and mean both “poison” (毒) and “to read” (読, pronounced ‘doku’ in certain contexts), a beautiful metaphor for how the ability to read, which grants access to knowledge, can be deemed as poisonous to the female mind.

The Apothecary Diaries rewrites the poison-woman trope. Maomao is a young woman deeply knowledgeable in toxicology, but she is not demonized for it. Rather, her expertise is portrayed as admirable and fundamentally useful, not as witchcraft. Maomao’s scientific curiosity is morally neutral—it’s part of who she is, a stem of her personality rooted in her apothecary adoptive father’s upbringing and the necessity of surviving the pleasure district. Rather than a threatening femme fatale, such as Cleopatra or Lucrezia Borgia, whose intellect and pharmacological mastery were framed as seductive and dangerous, Maomao comes across as a nerdy, somewhat antisocial girl who would rather experiment in a closet than attend a social event.

As a scientist, Maomao practices ethical calibration of her knowledge. She is willing to take risks upon herself that she would never inflict on others. Maomao has a habit of self-experimentation. She, on occasion, ingests small doses of poisons or tries experimental antidotes on herself to understand their effects. This almost reckless pursuit of knowledge on her own body shows her commitment to learning and her moral code, as she would rather suffer herself than accidentally poison someone else through ignorance. When ethical dilemmas arise in the palace cases, Maomao weighs her actions carefully. She often opts for quiet interventions, like delivering a secret note, whispering advice to a higher-up who can act, or making a cure and giving it to a patient without explanation. She is keenly aware that revealing the full truth can sometimes do more harm than good in a court where truth is not always welcome. For example, in the case of the sick imperial infants, she initially tries to stay anonymous, hoping the problem can be fixed without exposing anyone to punishment. When later pressed about how she knew that the leaded face powder was the culprit, Maomao deflects and downplays her own involvement. She constantly calculates the dosage of truth to find the right enough concentration to solve the crisis, but not so much that she or others will suffer backlash. Maomao’s goal is not to be right or to be praised but to ensure survival and a measure of justice with minimal collateral damage. 17

Through this narrative transformation, The Apothecary Diaries reclaims a figure historically used to warn against female expertise and recasts her as a practitioner of care. By embedding Maomao’s expertise within the ethics of dosage, the series dismantles the logic that equates female knowledge with danger. In doing so, the poison woman exists not as a cautionary tale, but as an apothecary.

3Maomao’s “Dosage” Reworks Female Archetypes

Maomao’s upbringing in the “toxic” environments has left her with a unique personal “dosage”, a carefully calibrated temperament that protects her like an antidote. If knowledge is poison in excess, she takes it in measured amounts. If beauty invites danger, she doses herself with plainness. She approaches life as an apothecary does a volatile mixture—balances ingredients and tests reactions. Maomao’s personality and relationships have been shaped by that logic of careful control. Her curiosity, her use of her body as a text and test subject all reflect Maomao not as a rebellious or flamboyant protagonist but a quiet, resilient observer.18

If the poison woman archetype frames female knowledge as dangerous, the female body becomes the surface on which that danger is read. In The Apothecary Diaries, Maomao’s body functions not as a passive object of interpretation, but as a text she actively edits. Her appearance, most notably her deliberate use of freckles, operates as a form of narrative control, allowing her to manage how she is seen and therefore how she is treated.

Naturally, Maomao could be considered an attractive young woman, but she has no interest in being seen as one. As a girl in the pleasure district, she realized that a pretty child could easily become prey. Using her apothecary skills, Maomao created a cosmetic solution to give herself freckles to dull her complexion, in effect, crafting a mask of “ugliness” to ward off unwanted attention. She hunches her posture and dresses plainly. By her own words, she became a “short, skinny, ugly girl” on purpose in a deliberate act of self-defense. This strategy situates Maomao within a longer tradition of women modifying their appearance as a form of self-protection rather than self-expression. Minori Ishida’s analysis of female characters in anime emphasizes how restraint and visual understatement can function as resistance to objectifying gazes, allowing women to retain agency without provoking punitive backlash.19 Maomao’s freckles operate precisely in this way. They do not erase her body, but they alter its legibility. She weaponizes the very tools of femininity, such as cosmetics and demeanor, not to allure, but to repel.

The series makes the stakes of this choice explicit through moments when Maomao’s disguise is removed. One of the most visually striking moments in the anime occurs in episode 5, where, on the eve of the emperor’s grand garden party, Lady Gyokuyou’s (玉葉) attendants insist on dollying Maomao up to be presentable. Maomao is to serve as Gyokuyou’s, a highly ranked consort’s, food taster at this public event, so she must look the part of an elegant lady-in-waiting. Despite Maomao’s grumbling, the other women scrub off her painted freckles and dress her in a fine pink gown to match her mistress’s colors. For the first time, the audience sees Maomao’s “true” face. The show pointedly unveils Maomao through the eyes of a male observer. As Jinshi (壬氏), a high-ranking palace official who oversees the inner court and serves as Maomao’s superior, approaches, the camera takes his perspective, panning up from Maomao’s hands to her face. In that reveal moment, she is unexpectedly breathtaking: her skin is smooth, her eyes accentuated by delicate makeup, and she wears the necklace Lady Gyokuyou gave her as a token of gratitude. The soundtrack shifts to elegant strings, underscoring the moment of refined beauty. It’s a scene straight out of a fairy tale or a romance anime, where the plain girl suddenly transforms into a beauty, and the handsome elite man is awestruck. For a brief second, the audience is invited to see what Jinshi sees: a young woman virtually unrecognizable from the freckled maid everyone knew. The episode does not let this seemingly magical transformation go unexamined. The anime contrasts the shōjo (少女, genres for young girls) scene with the image of a man’s foot stepping on a delicate flower in a moonlit alley. When the foot lifts, the flower is crushed and smeared. This is a poetic metaphor on how a beautiful, delicate flower can attract violence, leading to its demise. Maomao states this matter-of-factly: this is why she painted freckles on her face. She delivers the story with such calm detachment that Jinshi’s eyes widen in guilt and horror. For her, this trauma is simply part of life’s unfairness, not something to cry over or assign blame for.

In contrast to the involuntary makeover, the story offers a later scene where Maomao chooses to transform her appearance, this time as an act of personal expression. This occurs in the final, 24th, episode of season one, when, upon learning that her biological mother, Fengxian (鳳仙), whose mind has deteriorated due to advanced syphilis, is bought out and released from the pleasure district, Maomao decides to honor the courtesan’s tradition: the courtesans dance to send off their sister of the house, when she is bought out. Now, far away , in a private midnight moment in the imperial palace, Maomao performs that farewell dance alone atop a palace wall. This scene is rich with symbolic significance. First, Maomao removes her self-imposed ugliness by wiping away her freckles completely and dressing herself in splendid garments. Unlike the garden party, this makeover has no audience. Maomao embraces the full grace of her courtesan training, which she usually keeps under wraps. As the music swells, she elegantly dances under the night sky. The animation shows her sleeves and skirt flowing, her face serene yet melancholic. She looks like a flower in full bloom at the height of the night. Though Maomao would never call Fengxian “mother,” in this dance she conveys what words would not: compassion, sorrow, and forgiveness.

The palace-wall dance conveys the central contradictions of Maomao’s character. Throughout the series, she insists she is not troubled by her past and her trauma from being raised in the pleasure district. She consistently downplays her femininity and emotions, yet here she can shine as a figure of ethereal beauty and grace. This places Maomao in a liminal space between two worlds: she is both the rational apothecary of the inner palace and the flower bud of the hanamachi. The scene visually and narratively connects those spheres. Maomao engages in a tradition created by marginalized women (courtesans), showing her solidarity and gratitude to that lineage, while also making it her own personal act of catharsis. The dance is depicted almost as a sacred act, intimate and untouchable by the male gaze or the imperial power structure. This scene is splendid and mesmerizing. Here, the series acknowledges the courtesan’s art as something with dignity and emotional depth, not merely performative acts.

When interrupted by Jinshi, who had been alerted that a “ghost” was on the wall, Maomao swiftly reverted to her default mode. By this point, her leg wound has reopened from the exertion, and blood stains her dress. Maomao proceeds to start stitching up her own leg on the spot. The ethereal dancer vanishes, replaced by the pragmatic medic. The juxtaposition allows Maomao one night of vulnerability and expressive beauty, which she then immediately compartmentalizes and moves on. The palace wall dance remains her hitorigoto, her private monologue in motion. Japanese media scholars have noted that moments of nonverbal expression in women-centered narratives often serve as spaces of autonomy, allowing characters to articulate interior states without submitting them to judgment.20 Maomao’s dance functions in this register.

These two scenes—the garden party makeover and the palace wall dance—demonstrate how Maomao’s control over her own appearance is integral to her agency. Whether it is disguising herself to avoid harm or briefly revealing herself to express emotion, Maomao understands her body as a text that she can edit. She is keenly aware of how others “read” a woman’s looks, and she exploits that. Maomao can scrub off her freckles and dance magnificently when the occasion calls for it, but she can also put the freckles back on and blend into the background to get her real work done. Her true strength lies in knowing when to hide and when to shine.21 22

Maomao stands out against the backdrop of both classical literary archetypes and modern anime tropes. She is decidedly not a typical shōjo heroine or even a conventional “strong female character” as seen in many action/fantasy series. There is a running joke among fans encapsulated by a YouTube comment under the anime scene of Maomao’s palace wall dance, “Maomao in this scene: graceful like a swan. Maomao throughout most of the show: unhinged gremlin.” Indeed, Maomao’s moments of elegance or romantic softness are fleeting. Most of the time, she is blunt, obsessed with her experiments, and comically unfazed by things that would make a proper lady gasp. This makes her a breath of fresh air for audiences tired of mass-produced protagonists.23

Firstly, Maomao is not naive. Many heroines of palace dramas or historical romances begin as wide-eyed innocents who learn the harshness of court life over time. Maomao, by contrast, enters the story already jaded and worldly-wise in her own peculiar way. Having grown up in the pleasure district, nothing about human vice or suffering really surprises her. She responds to poisonings, betrayals, and the volatility of imperial favor with practiced detachment. This sets her apart from the typical audience-surrogate character who discovers intrigues along with the audience. Unlike heroines such as Sakura Haruno from Naruto, who begin inexperienced and gradually harden through loss and failure, Maomao enters the narrative already disillusioned. Maomao is ahead of the game from the beginning, and the audience is catching up to her understanding of how ugly the world can be.

Secondly, Maomao is not driven by romance or male validation. In many stories set in courts or harems, the heroine’s arc is tied to a grand love story or a love triangle, her agency often entwined with choosing or being chosen by a man of high status. Maomao exists outside of that economy of desire. In many shōjo stories, characters like early-mentioned Sakura Haruno are repeatedly framed through romantic longing and emotional dependence, but Maomao’s motivations remain detached from male validation. Although she serves an imperial consort and interacts closely with Jinshi, her motivations are strikingly independent of any romantic yearning. She solves mysteries because she’s curious or because people’s lives are at stake, not to impress a love interest. As long as she had a supply of time and ingredients, Maomao would continue contentedly mixing herbs and cracking cases. This is not to say she stays oblivious to all attraction, as there are subtle hints she finds Jinshi beautiful, and she certainly enjoys good food and comforts, but she decides not to dwell on these things. When Jinshi flirts or when other girls gossip about handsome men, Maomao’s reaction ranges from puzzled to apathetic. This lack of romantic agenda is a significant departure from genre norms.

Maomao’s humor and personality also defy the kawaii, or “cute”, stereotype often expected of female leads. Unlike kawaii-coded heroines such as Usagi Tsukino from Sailor Moon, whose emotional expressiveness and vulnerability are central to their appeal, Maomao’s detachment and bluntness resist cuteness as a narrative expectation. She is not clumsy in an adorable way, or sweetly sassy, or passionately stoic. Instead, Maomao’s sense of humor is dry and sometimes grim. She cackles to herself when she gets excited about a poison. She can be socially tone-deaf, delivering blunt observations that either offend or bewilder more refined company. In one episode, while observing a corpse, she casually mentions that she was forbidden by her adoptive father to touch and dissect a dead body because human remains can be used in some medicine, completely missing the horror on her listeners’ faces. The show often plays these moments for laughs, not at Maomao’s expense but as a quirky charm: the heroine is a “gremlin” who delights in repulsive matters in her spare time. This comedic framing further sets her apart from the saintly and virtuous heroines or the ultra-competent superheroines in other series. Maomao’s competence is balanced by her eccentricity, making her feel more human.

Another way she subverts tropes is through her emotional guardedness. Modern media has trended toward portraying “strong female characters” who nonetheless have a heart of gold and readily form found families or open up emotionally by the end of their journey. Maomao is strong, yes, and compassionate in action, but emotionally, she remains somewhat closed off. This means she can come off as unsympathetic or cold to those around her and to some viewers expecting a more bubbly or passionate lead. But The Apothecary Diaries understands the trauma and context behind Maomao’s reserve. It does not frame her as needing to be “fixed” by love or friendship, but rather, treats her wariness as reasonable. Over time, she does warm to certain people, but even then, her affection is shown in subtle ways. This realistic pacing of character growth defies the typical neat character arc where the heroine “learns to love” or overcomes a single character flaw.

Maomao’s strength as a female protagonist lies in her being resilient, competent, and unapologetically odd. In a medium where female characters are often forced into either the girlboss or femme fatale archetype, Maomao follows a third path. She is simply Maomao, a woman shaped by hardship into a resourceful individual who neither seeks permission nor fights for liberation. The story fully embraces her “gremlin” energy and invites the audience to love her not in spite of it, but because of it.

Conclusion

The Apothecary Diaries offers an exploration of a woman navigating and subtly subverting oppressive systems, carving out autonomy through the very tools those systems give and deny. Thematically, the series shows that agency for women does not always appear as rebellion. It can manifest as endurance, adaptation, and quiet transformation of one’s circumstances. Maomao does not overthrow the patriarchy of her world, but she does undermine it in small yet significant ways. She saves lives that would have been lost to ignorance or malice and brings a measure of justice or closure where there would have been none. She navigates class divides, going from a lowly servant to a valued unofficial court pharmacist, without ever seeking rank. Through Maomao’s eyes, the audience sees how women in history might not have had official power, but they exercised influence through knowledge, relationships, and resilience.24

In reimagining literary archetypes, The Apothecary Diaries contributes to a broader redefinition of female protagonists in fiction. It argues that a heroine can be compelling and strong without fitting the mold of a warrior or a romantic lead. She can be plain, she can be odd, she can care more about toxins than marriage, and her story can still grip millions. The popularity of the novels, manga, and anime suggests that audiences are ready for such narratives. As one critic puts it, The Apothecary Diaries is “an incomparable remedy for tired tropes”.25 It offers something at once new and comfortingly familiar: a tale of palace intrigue and peril, but told with the voice of a smart, unorthodox woman who, rather than being another flower on display, acts like an herb in the shade. The story fully embraces her “gremlin” energy and invites the audience to love her not in spite of it, but because of it. In reclaiming figures such as the poison woman, the courtesan, and the palace servant from narratives of danger or disposability, The Apothecary Diaries performs an act of cultural recovery. It restores value to forms of knowledge and care historically dismissed as feminine or unremarkable.

Maomao’s journey invites the audience to reconsider what forms female strength can take. She is a wildflower that learned to grow in the shadows of the garden, unnoticed by most. Yet in that wildflower lies a potent medicine. Maomao navigates systems that would rather silence her, and in doing so, she quietly ensures that some flowers bloom to survive, not just to be seen. The symbolic language of the wood sorrel flower acts as a metaphor: just like this low-growing herb wildflower, Maomao thrives by enduring in overlooked spaces. Just as some flowers are meant to grow in shade, some stories, long overlooked, bloom precisely because they do.
