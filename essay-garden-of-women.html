A Garden of Women

Poison, Insects, and Forms of Female Agency in The Apothecary Diaries

Field
East Asian Languages & Cultures
Advisor
Andy Rodekohr
Completed
May 2026
Institution
Wake Forest University
Abstract

The Apothecary Diaries (Kusuriya no hitorigoto 薬屋のひとりごと), a light novel series by Hyūga Natsu 日向夏 that has expanded into hugely popular manga and anime adaptations, centers on Maomao, a courtesan-house apothecary turned imperial palace servant who has become an unconventional heroine icon. Her detective abilities allow her to navigate court conspiracies through forensic observation, calibrated disclosure, and strategic management of her own visibility. This thesis project reads The Apothecary Diaries against Japanese and Chinese literary and cultural traditions to argue that it rewrites historically gendered archetypes by reconstructing female agency not as rebellion but as a practice of dosage: knowing precisely how much to reveal, and when. Maomao sits at the tension between two identities, neither the court nor literary history can reconcile: the apothecary and the courtesan. The series dismantles the dokufu 毒婦, or poison woman, archetype—the cultural equation of female toxicological knowledge with moral corruption. Her expertise is deployed ethically, her curiosity is eccentric rather than sinister, and her narrative mode, situated within the Japanese nikki 日記 diary tradition, constructs authority through selective disclosure and material evidence rather than institutional position. Where Maomao’s method is forensic and grounded, the maidservant Shisui’s is literary and metaphorical, encoded through ghost story indirection, layered camouflage, and spider-web networks of lateral power. Their dynamic reveals the full range of strategies The Apothecary Diaries proposes for women trapped inside masculine systems designed to consume them.

Introduction

Every poison has a dose that kills and a dose that heals. In the imperial, China-esque world of The Apothecary Diaries (Kusuriya no hitorigoto 薬屋のひとりごと), beauty, obedience, and knowledge also function according to this logic. Truth must be diluted, curiosity disguised, and emotion administered only in trace amounts. Women in this world are cultivated like flowers: trained, displayed, evaluated, and discarded once damaged or depleted. Both courtesans and concubines are grown for use and prized for their appearance. The servants who attend these women are trained to feign ignorance, but Maomao (猫猫), an apothecary turned servant, breaks that rule. She watches, calculates, and reads what others overlook: the shimmer of lead beneath powder and the dangers blooming quietly under silk. She knows that survival depends not on blooming brightest, but on knowing precisely when not to bloom at all.1

The Apothecary Diaries began as a web novel on Shosetsuka ni Naro (小説家になろう) in 2011 by Hyūga Natsu 日向夏. Shufunotomo (主婦の友社) acquired and published it as a light novel series beginning in 2014, illustrated by Touko Shino (しのとうこ). As of the end of 2025, the series has published 16 volumes and sold over 14 million copies. Two concurrent manga adaptations, launched in 2017, together exceed 22 million copies sold. The anime adaptation premiered in 2023, became one of the highest-rated series of its year, and returned for a second season in 2024. A third season and an original story film are slated for the latter half of 2026. These combined successes reflect the appeal of the story and its characters, especially the main protagonist, Maomao. Set in a fictional empire inspired by late-imperial China—primarily drawing on the Tang dynasty (618–907) and incorporating scholarly knowledge from the late Ming dynasty (1368–1644)—the narrative blends court intrigue, medical investigation, and character-driven mystery, where Maomao uses her pharmacological training and pleasure district upbringing to navigate the plot’s central conflicts. The world the series constructs draws on both periods with different purposes: Tang-dynasty Chang’an (長安), whose pleasure quarters are the cultural ancestor of the hanamachi, provides the aesthetic and entertainment register Maomao is raised in while the late Ming dynasty with its Forbidden City (紫禁城) provides the pharmacological knowledge and palace hierarchy the narrative setting.

Maomao grows up knowing early on that beauty attracts danger, knowledge invites punishment, and the wrong concentrations of honesty can ruin a life faster than poison. The story begins with Maomao being kidnapped and sold into service at the imperial palace. She initially seeks invisibility, intending to endure her term quietly; however, that strategy collapses when Maomao, retaining her curious and eccentric personality, deduces that the emperor’s newborn children are being poisoned by leaded face powder used by their mothers. Her anonymous intervention saves their lives but exposes her literacy and medical competence, drawing her into the palace’s inner workings. Maomao does not see this visibility as empowerment. She attempts to erase her role, understanding that knowledge wielded too openly becomes a liability. As Hyūga Natsu has explained in interviews, the series is intentionally written with ambiguity and restraint, allowing knowledge to surface only in partial, controlled forms so that it remains ethical rather than destructive.2 This philosophy structures both Maomao’s actions and the narrative itself.

Season one, covering the first two volumes of the light novel, traces Maomao’s entry into the inner court. Interpreting her position with sharp precision from the very first episode, she describes the rear palace as “a garden of women who are to give birth to the emperor’s children”—“a huge collective of two thousand concubines and servants, and one thousand eunuchs,” in which serving girls like Maomao are “expendable and can lose their lives at any moment.”3 Her fast acclimatization can be attributed to her pleasure district (hanamachi 花街)4 upbringing, and as the story unfolds, she gradually emerges from invisibility.

Season two expands the scope considerably, moving deeper into the imperial palace conspiracies, giving Maomao more mobility across the different courts, widening the cast of women, and introducing Shisui (子翠), a new maidservant whose insect obsession, hidden identity, and ultimate metamorphosis reframe the series’ central questions about what survival looks like for women trapped inside masculine systems designed to consume them.

The series does more than present unconventional heroines: Maomao functions as a reclamation figure, carrying forward forms of female agency that literature and history have often allowed to stay in the margins and wither; and Shisui demonstrates that transformation itself can be a form of power, that shedding one’s name and face is not destruction but emergence. This thesis reads The Apothecary Diaries against Japanese and Chinese literary and cultural traditions to argue that it rewrites historically gendered archetypes by reconstructing female agency not as rebellion but as a practice of dosage and a navigation of networks.

Together, Maomao’s and Shisui’s methods map the full range of survival strategies available to women within the imperial Sino-Japanese hierarchies.

1Toxic Gardens and Walled Courts

Maomao’s first reading of the imperial rear palace as “a garden of women who are to give birth to the emperor’s children” names a precise logic she already knows. The hanamachi (花街), the pleasure district where she grows up, runs on the same grammar: women are cultivated for use, evaluated at peak, and discarded once depleted. Neither space acts as purely a background. Both are frames that enclose the action and define the rules—the walled courts where the constraints both Maomao and Shisui navigate take shape and become, for the women inside them, simply the nature of things.

In the hanamachi, femininity is not a personal attribute but a trained performance. Beauty functions as currency and exposure: the more visible a woman becomes, the more fully she is made available for purchase and harm. The hanamachi operates by dosage—courtesans learn how much charm to offer, how much emotion to simulate, and how much of themselves to reveal to survive without being consumed. Girls enter pleasure houses as kamuro (稿), child attendants who learn by observing and serving the senior courtesans above them. Their training covers dance, music, poetry, posture, witty conversation, and everything designed to attract and entertain their elite male clientele. Courtesans are appraised by the rokuyoku (六欲), the “six attractions”: color, or complexion and beauty; form, or figure and bearing; carriage, or grace in movement; voice, in speech and song; softness, or tenderness of demeanor; and features, facial beauty taken as a whole.5 This mirrors the refinements of aristocratic literati culture in structure while inverting its purpose: where noblemen cultivate poetry and calligraphy to gain social standing, courtesans cultivate the same arts to constitute their market value. Young girls are sorted at entry and re-evaluated at peak bloom; those who do not meet the standard are relegated to lower roles, a brutal arithmetic that a child of the hanamachi, like Maomao, witnesses from childhood. Calling the pleasure district a “flower town” frames coercion as cultivation and exploitation as something natural.

The wings of a theater stage are where performers wait before their time comes. There, they stand—present, prepared, fully formed—but not yet visible to the audience. The wings are not simply the edge of the stage, but its necessary infrastructure. Women in the hanamachi occupy exactly this position: trained into a readiness the system defined and held at the margin until the moment of display arrives. Maomao grows up in those “wings” and learns to navigate them with precision.

Yet the hanamachi is not rendered as a site of pure victimhood. Within its constraints, women develop lateral forms of agency through artistry, reputation, and mutual dependence.

Widmer’s study of late-imperial courtesan writers shows how performance could become a platform for cultural authority, allowing some women to speak, circulate texts, and cultivate influence within systems designed to commodify them.6 The Tang-dynasty poet and courtesan Xue Tao (薇涛, c. 768–831) is one such figure: registered with the guild of entertainers in Chengdu, she became one of the four great female poets of her era, and her handmade stationery—small, ornate sheets now known as Xue Tao Jian (薇涛笺)—circulated among literati long after her own position had changed.7 What she made within constraint outlasted the constraint itself.

The hanamachi also shapes Maomao’s relationship to knowledge in a more specific way.

In a space that expects women to be decorative rather than competent, medical expertise is treated as destabilizing. Marran’s analysis of the dokufu (毒婦, “poison woman”) archetype clarifies this fear: women associated with bodily knowledge—particularly toxins—are consistently framed as seductive, deviant, or dangerous, their expertise re-coded as a moral threat rather than a practical skill.8 Maomao inherits this kind of knowledge, but refuses the narrative that would make her spectacular. She treats knowledge as responsibility rather than power. This early education forms the foundation for her later survival in the palace. Before imperial authority ever attempts to regulate her, the hanamachi has already taught her the rules: be useful, not ornamental; observe without announcing; give only what is required. The flower town exists to cultivate women for sale. Maomao learns how to remain unsold.

If the hanamachi trains women for display and eventual sale, the imperial rear palace refines that same logic under the language of virtue, harmony, and dynastic order. When Maomao is kidnapped and sold into palace service, she finds that this gilded world is simply the hanamachi in another dress. Like courtesans, the emperor’s consorts are ranked and evaluated relentlessly. Bossler’s scholarship on gender and court culture in late imperial China identifies the organizing principle: women’s value in palace systems is tied to their capacity to produce heirs while remaining ideologically pure, ornamentally presentable, and politically inert.9 A consort who falls out of favor might as well wither like a cut flower. The harmony of the rear palace, like the harmony of the pleasure quarter, depends on women’s exquisite obedience, charm, and submission. The palace does not civilize the flower economy. It perfects it.

The poisoned infants case makes this logic literal. The court’s insistence on idealized beauty—the use of leaded white face powder as a requirement of feminine presentation—transforms aesthetic discipline into biological harm. The system that prizes appearance above all else poisons its own reproductive future. Beauty ceases to be symbolic and becomes directly destructive. Maomao’s response is equally revealing: she writes a warning rather than confronting authority directly, calibrating her intervention to minimize exposure. The palace expects women of her rank to be interchangeable and silent. Her competence is already a disruption simply by existing.

The palace’s medical hierarchy reinforces this boundary. Formal medicine belongs to male eunuch physicians sanctioned by the court, while women’s medical knowledge is tolerated only when it remains domestic, invisible, or derivative.10 A woman who knows too much is threatening, regardless of whether that knowledge could save lives. Maomao’s expertise occupies an unstable position: she possesses skill without institutional legitimacy. She decides the safest course is to stay silent about the full extent of what she knows.

Both the hanamachi and the rear palace are, in this sense, walled courts: enclosed spaces of cultivation where beauty is enforced, knowledge is suspect, and compliance is the price of survival. The garden produces flowers for display. What it cannot see is the women who have learned, within its own walls, to read the system that raised them.

2Maomao’s Method

In conventional detective stories, the investigator’s authority derives from institutional position, exceptional intelligence, or the permission of those in power: Sherlock Holmes is consulted, and Hercule Poirot is invited to solve the mystery. Their methods may be eccentric, but their right to investigate is never in serious doubt. Maomao’s character has none of this. She is a palace servant who was purchased, not hired, and her competence is structurally inconvenient in a court where female expertise is not expected. Her method of solving cases is, therefore, a way of existing inside a system that has no authorized place for what she knows. What she knows, specifically, is the language of bodies and substances. She has no formal training—her knowledge was accumulated in the pleasure district, where Maomao’s adoptive father worked as a doctor and an apothecary. The hanamachi taught her that cosmetics, scent, bodily functions, and disease were not peripheral concerns but central ones.11 By the time Maomao enters the imperial palace, she carries knowledge acquired from marginalized spaces that allows her different perspectives on the court’s ritual processes. This expertise is foreign to the imperial system, which does not pay attention to what happens at the margins.

Maomao’s investigative method follows a consistent pattern regular enough to call a “signature.” First, she notices something others have categorized as irrelevant or invisible: a discoloration, a smell, an inconsistency in a symptom. Then she quietly tests hypotheses without announcing her theory and identifies the cause through material evidence. Finally and crucially, she decides how much of her conclusion to share, with whom, and in what form.

Maomao consistently understands that the truth, administered without calibration, can cause as much damage as poison. When she deduces that the imperial infants are being poisoned by the lead in their mothers’ face powder, she does not go directly to the emperor or confront the consorts. She writes an anonymous note, calculated to reach the relevant parties through marginal channels while protecting herself from exposure. When she is later pressed to explain how she knew, she deflects, underplays, and redirects. This evasiveness reflects a clear understanding that her institutional position offers no protection for her competence. The palace’s medical hierarchy assigns formal expertise to male eunuch physicians, while women’s medical knowledge is not usually tolerated. Maomao navigates this constraint by routing around it, delivering conclusions without the spelled-out credentials. She is realistic about what the system permits and acts where action is possible, withholds where withholding is safer, and measures every intervention by its probable consequences.12

This logic is not only historical. In 2018, it became known that Tokyo Medical University and several other Japanese medical schools had for years systematically altered women’s entrance examination scores downward, on the grounds that women were more likely to leave the profession after marriage or childbirth.13 The mechanism differs from Maomao’s court, but the assumption is the same: female medical competence is treated as a problem to be managed rather than a resource to be developed.

Central to Maomao’s method is her relationship to the body as a site of evidence. The imperial court treats the female body as a medium for aesthetic display and social performance. Cosmetics, clothing, posture, and demeanor are all legible as status, while illness and physical deterioration are managed as disruptions. Maomao reads the body entirely differently: for her, it is a text, and the symptoms it produces are data.

Maomao’s expertise in toxicology places her in a tradition the series is consciously engaging with. In Japanese literary and cultural history, the figure of the dokufu (毒婦, “poison woman”) condenses a particular set of anxieties about female knowledge. Christine Marran traces this archetype across modern Japanese culture, showing how women associated with poisons—whether as practitioners, poisoners, or objects of suspicion—are consistently framed as threats to social order rather than as skilled practitioners of pharmacology.14 The logic is that poison knowledge in a woman is evidence of moral corruption, and moral corruption makes the knowledge dangerous. The fact that the knowledge might be practically valuable, medically sophisticated, or ethically deployed is outside the archetype’s function.

There is an additional linguistic dimension in the Japanese context worth noting. The word doku carries a double meaning: it means “poison” (毒) but is also a homophone to doku meaning “to read” (読) in certain grammatical constructions. The ability to read, which grants access to knowledge and to text, is itself encoded as a form of contamination that can poison a woman’s mind. However, The Apothecary Diaries systematically rewrites this narrative. It portrays Maomao as knowledgeable, literate, and an expert in toxicology, and none of these things acts as evidence of her moral corruption. Her curiosity is rendered as eccentric rather than sinister, her self-experimentation as committed rather than deviant, and her occasional misjudgments as genuinely human rather than as proof of a corrupting ambition.

The series’ most resonant historical parallel may be Tan Yunxian (談允賢, 1461–1556), a female physician of the Ming dynasty—the same period the narrative borrows from most heavily for its pharmacological knowledge and palace hierarchy—who compiled the Nǜyī zayan (女醫 雜言, “Miscellaneous Records of a Female Doctor”) in 1511. The text contains thirty-one case histories: her diagnoses and herbal treatments for gynecological, postpartum, and pediatric conditions, the chronic and gendered ailments that formal medicine preferred to ignore. Tan Yunxian received her medical training not through any institutional channel but inherited it from her paternal grandmother who came from a family of physicians, and did not begin practicing in earnest until she fell ill herself. She treated women who would not see male doctors, built her expertise through observation and documentation, and produced the only surviving female-authored medical text of the Ming dynasty, published by her son after her death.15 The parallel to Maomao is striking: knowledge acquired outside institutions and applied to conditions the establishment dismissed.

Maomao’s narrative mode extends this rewriting into literary form. Her perspective throughout the series is that of an observer who narrates what she notices to herself—a private account, unperformed and selective, in the register of someone speaking aloud in an empty room. This connection is built into the series’ Japanese title: Kusuriya no Hitorigoto (薬屋のひとりご と), which literally means “The Apothecary’s Soliloquy.” The word hitorigoto (独り言 or 一人言, speaking to oneself) is parallel to the act of a diary-writer recording private thoughts and observations that have no intended audience.

Edith Sarra’s analysis of Heian court women’s writing emphasizes that court women’s texts construct narrative power through selective disclosure, using indirection as a deliberate strategy and not a sign of ignorance or passivity.16 Women write from the margins between public and private, where the observation itself becomes a form of control. The influence of Japanese court women’s memoirs can be seen in how Maomao’s narration is constructed: she is frank about bodily realities—like menstruation, injury, or the messiness of childbirth—and documents emotional undertones without open judgment, similar to how Heian women diarists would matter-of-factly note their joys and sorrows of court life.

Maomao’s narrative operates in a liminal space between two worlds: she is both the rational apothecary of the inner palace and the flower bud of the hanamachi. In one scene, by dancing on the palace wall, Maomao engages in a tradition created by marginalized women—courtesans—showing her solidarity and belonging to that lineage.17 The dance is a sacred act, intimate and untouchable by the male gaze or the imperial power structure, and it remains her hitorigoto, her private monologue.18 Here, the series acknowledges the courtesan’s art as something with dignity and emotional depth. This moment of nonverbal expression carves out the space for Maomao-the-courtesan in the imperial “garden of women,” where she is predominantly known as Maomao-the-apothecary.

Maomao’s method is organized around endurance within constraints, not transformation beyond them. She can reduce harm, expose certain kinds of corruption, and navigate hierarchies with competence. What she cannot do—and what her entire mode of self-presentation is organized to avoid—is change the system. She enters the palace as someone who has cultivated invisibility, and even as her position shifts, she remains oriented toward the same goals: solve the problem, stay out of range, and never reveal the full extent of what she knows, acting as a quiet, resilient observer.1920

3Petals and Wings

The women of The Apothecary Diaries inhabit two symbolic registers, though the boundary between them is neither fixed nor absolute. The first is visible and ornamental: these women are flowers, cultivated for display, fragrant while in season, and expected to wither gracefully when their use has passed. The second is less legible to the systems that surround it: these women have learned to move through the world like insects, using camouflage, specialized knowledge, and metamorphosis to persist in environments not designed with their survival in mind. Both categories operate in the same garden. Insects emerge from flowers—whether through metamorphosis or through the labor of others of their kind—and the flower-insect relationship is one of mutual dependence even when it looks like opposition. The categories describe modes of being, and many characters occupy both simultaneously or shift between them as circumstances change. The symbolic framework the series builds around flowers and insects is consistent and culturally grounded enough to constitute an argument about what forms female power and agency can take.

The floral logic is not merely decorative: it is structural. The pleasure district operates by exactly this grammar—women cultivated, evaluated at bloom, and discarded when past season.

What the imperial palace adds is not a different logic but a more elaborate one. Cecilia Segawa Seigle’s study of the Yoshiwara identifies this garden economy as an embedded system, where young women enter as seedlings, undergo formal training, and are appraised at their “bloom” for peak commercial or reproductive value.21 Beverly Jo Bossler’s scholarship on imperial Chinese court culture extends the same logic into the harem structure, where consorts are ranked according to their ability to produce heirs while remaining ornamentally presentable and politically inert.22 In both contexts, the flower is valued for its appearance and compliance: it does not move and does not choose when to bloom. This system appears in the world of The Apothecary Diaries as simply the nature of things, which is the part that makes it legible as a system rather than a backdrop.

When flowers accumulate too much—too much knowledge, too much ambition, too much refusal to wither—they become something else in the cultural imagination. Christine Marran’s analysis of the poison woman identifies this moment of transformation as the site where the ornamental feminine becomes what she calls the predatory feminine: a woman whose femininity itself has become dangerous, whose beauty is now understood as a weapon, and whose intelligence is now a form of contamination.23 Several powerful women in The Apothecary Diaries’ background—the founding empress, the emperor’s deceased mother, Loulan’s mother Shenmei—are constructed as having operated through exactly this register: strategic adaptation, alliance-building, and camouflage over fixed position and ornamental display. Their methods are consistently framed as threatening, dangerous, and ultimately unsustainable. The grammar that encodes this threat is the grammar of the predatory feminine.

The most explicit historical reference point that encapsulates this logic is Wu Zetian (武 則天, 624–705 CE), the only woman in Chinese history to rule as emperor in her own name. Wu Zetian’s historical reputation was constructed almost entirely through the lens of the predatory feminine: she was accused of infanticide, of eliminating rivals with brutal efficiency, and of acquiring power through seduction before deploying it through terror. Whether these accusations reflect historical reality or the anxieties of male officials who could not process female sovereignty except through a grammar of monstrosity is a question historians continue to debate. What is not in question is the grammar itself: a woman who moved through the world like an insect—adapting through transformation and alliance rather than through fixed position and ornamental display—was encoded as a threat to the natural order. The series uses her as a distant cautionary mirror, the endpoint of what the predatory feminine looks like when allowed to run its full course.

To understand what insects mean in The Apothecary Diaries, it is necessary to understand what they have historically meant in the cultural traditions the series draws on. Chinese literary and ritual culture does not treat insects as a uniform category of minor creatures. Individual insects carry specific and often contradictory symbolic loads, and each load has something to say about the women who embody them.

The cicada is perhaps the richest single example. In the Han dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) burial practice, jade cicadas were placed on the tongues of the deceased to express the hope of rebirth—encoding the cicada’s life cycle as a model for the afterlife: years underground before emergence, the shedding of the larval skin, and the brief, brilliant flight of the adult form.24 As Wilt Idema’s study documents, the cicada’s symbolic register in classical poetry extends further: its perch in high treetops and its diet of dew associated it with the refined official, while its autumn silence became a figure for mortality and cyclical return.25

The spider operates in yet another register. In Chinese vernacular narrative tradition, as Idema documents, the spider appears predominantly as a seductive female: a figure whose web is the trap, and whose beauty is indistinguishable from her danger.26 In premodern Chinese natural philosophy, spiders were classified alongside insects rather than distinguished from them; the taxonomic separation a biologist would observe was not operative in a tradition that organized the natural world by symbolic resonance. As a feminized figure in literary texts, the spider-woman represents the most extreme version of Marran’s predatory femininity. This framework matters for what follows: the structural logic of the web—invisible, centered on a figure who appears peripheral, maintained through continuous labor—resurfaces in the way Shisui organizes power.

The glowing insect completes this vocabulary. Where the cicada speaks to radical metamorphosis and the spider to networked, hidden power, the bioluminescent moth names the creature that produces its own light only in darkness, invisible by day, luminous only when everything else has gone dark. The hotaru (螢, firefly) carries this symbolic weight across both literary traditions the series draws on. In Japanese poetry, from the Man’yōshū through Heian court literature, it is a figure for passionate love and the souls of the dead—heat that is visible only after dark.27 The firefly chapter of The Tale of Genji uses the insect’s own light to expose a woman without her consent, illuminating what propriety keeps hidden in daylight: the glowing creature as an instrument of male curiosity that the woman cannot control. The Chinese register is different but structurally related. The story of Che Yin (車菸), who caught fireflies in gauze to read by when he could not afford oil for a lamp, encodes the glowing insect as the vehicle for knowledge acquired without institutional access, improvised from available darkness.

The Apothecary Diaries draws both registers into a single scene. In volume three of the light novel, Maomao learns from Shisui that certain moths are bioluminescent and that male moths are drawn to the pheromones of females.28 She uses this knowledge to reconstruct the “Moon Fairy,” a courtesan from the Verdigris House who half a century earlier had accidentally attracted swarms of glowing moths when a rival smeared dead female moths on her costume as a prank. The transcendent spectacle of a woman wreathed in floating light, beautiful enough, in Maomao’s own phrasing, to topple empires was the unintended product of insect pheromone biology. Maomao learns the mechanism from Shisui and deploys it, staging a recreation for foreign envoys using the moths.

Three things converge at this moment: Shisui’s entomological expertise is the practical engine of the solution, her knowledge that others might dismiss is the knowledge that ultimately works; the original Moon Fairy was a courtesan whose transcendence was imposed by observers; and the light that created the myth of divine feminine beauty is produced not by divinity but by the mating biology of moths. The garden cultivates flowers. The insects make them glow in the dark.

The Japanese literary tradition offers a parallel counterpoint. The Heian-period tale “The Lady Who Loved Insects,” from Tsutsumi Chunagon Monogatari, stages a young noblewoman who refuses the conventional feminine cultivation of butterflies in favor of caterpillars, crickets, and other despised creatures. Her preference is explicitly framed as a rejection of ornamental surfaces in favor of natural truth: “Why do people make so much fuss about butterflies and never give a thought to the creatures out of which butterflies grow? It is the natural form of things that is always the most important.”29 The lady does not pluck her eyebrows, does not blacken her teeth, and refuses to perform the conventional femininity her social position demands. When pressed, she argues that butterflies are merely “the sheddings of creatures like my hairy caterpillars, who discard them as a snake drops its skin.”30 What interests her is the form that is still becoming, not the form that has already arrived. This is precisely the epistemology the tale shares with Shisui: both locate their authority in the creature in the process of becoming, not the creature that has already bloomed.

What insects provide, across both traditions, is a symbolic vocabulary: transformation over stasis, marginality over display, collective survival over individual ornament, and a mode of power that operates through what is unnoticed. Aya Kitamura’s analysis of gender performance in Japanese cultural contexts identifies how women who operate through fluid, shifting, and ambivalent enactments of identity—neither fully compliant nor fully resistant—open up spaces of agency precisely through that instability.31

Both Maomao and Shisui revise the femme fatale archetype from the inside. Maomao’s revision is that of the poison woman rewritten as an apothecary—her toxicological expertise reframed as ethical care. Shisui’s revision is different, and it requires the insect framework to become fully legible. In The Apothecary Diaries, insects are not simply symbols of the predatory feminine. They are symbols of transformation, knowledge, collective survival, and marginal power, which can be predatory or can be something else, depending on who is wielding them.

4Shisui’s Method

In Chinese ghost literature, as Judith T. Zeitlin has argued, the female revenant draws on a posthumous power that living women cannot access: her self-determination exceeds that of living women precisely because she is no longer subject to the systems that regulated her.32 Ellen Widmer’s work on late Ming courtesan culture similarly identifies how women operating in constrained social spaces cultivate alternative registers of authority, influence built through network, reputation, and the circulation of knowledge across informal channels.33 Shisui’s arc extends this logic into the insect register.

Maomao meets Shisui at the inner palace in season two. While chasing a stray cat, Maomao is unexpectedly caught off guard—the cat is already in the hands of a young laundry maidservant, who offers it back with a cheerful smile and disappears before Maomao can ask her name. This is Shisui’s introduction: present at exactly the right moment, performing exactly the necessary function, and gone before she can be properly situated. What Maomao registers first is not a threat or a mystery but a personality—carefree and genuine, disarmingly so.

Loulan (樓蘭, Rouran), the Pure Consort, is introduced as a replacement for Ah-Duo, the previous Pure Consort who lost ability to have children, in the imperial inner court. As a consort, she is aloof and enigmatic, never speaking during the lecture Maomao is summoned to give the four high consorts, never sharing in the others’ amusement, and maintaining an expression of composed distance. Her wardrobe is excessive even by consort standards, her makeup always heavy and constantly changing—a detail that reads, in retrospect, as camouflage because Shisui is Loulan. The series discloses this towards the end of season two, and it reframes every scene she appears in. The architecture of her concealment is layered: rotating cosmetics and styles ensure no consistent impression of her face settles in anyone’s memory, and several of her ladies-in-waiting are trained to impersonate her so that her absence from her pavilion does not alarm anyone. But she does not merely make herself less visible or construct a second self whose only purpose is concealment. The carefree maidservant who loves bugs is not a mask over a real person. It is, in a sense, a real person who happens also to be a consort. Both selves are real. The camouflage does not conceal something for the sake of concealment only.

Where Maomao reads the palace as a pharmacologist reads a body, Shisui reads it the way a classical Chinese court poet reads a text: obliquely, through layers of allusion and metaphor, attentive to what the surface conceals. The two methods are epistemologically opposite and yet are part of the same structure, echoing each other as a counterpoint: one scientific and forensic, the other literary and interpretive. Maomao’s authority is forensic: she knows because she has observed, tested, and eliminated alternatives. Her conclusions are grounded in what the body produces. This groundedness is also a constraint: Maomao can only argue what she can observe and demonstrate. Shisui operates in the register that Maomao cannot. Her method is literary, her arguments embedded in metaphor, and her truths structured so that they can only be understood by those with the skills to interpret them—those who have the “dictionary” to read them.

When Shisui tells her ghost story at the candle-lit gathering, she does not explain her situation but encodes it entirely in the metaphor of the story—a method that only becomes fully legible at the end of season two. Twelve women gather in a closed room to tell tales by candlelight, each blowing out her candle at the story’s end. The gathering is a female narrative space organized around oral transmission, its stories moving outside the channels through which the court controls what can be officially known. Ghost stories in East Asian literary tradition have long functioned as spaces where trauma, suppressed knowledge, and social critique could be articulated under the protective cover of the supernatural.34 Shisui’s story is set in the distant east: a monk seeking shelter for the night is housed by a young couple, and through the thin walls, he hears the husband’s voice sounding like the chime of an insect. When the wife opens the monk’s door holding a knife, he does not see a human shadow but a cricket’s. He survives by continuing to chant. The story is distinctive in the episode because it is the one Maomao does not explain afterward. As Edith Sarra has argued, the mode of indirect disclosure—using the margins, the oblique, the embedded—is not a sign of limitation but a deliberate strategy through which women construct narrative authority from positions that formal discourse does not recognize.35

Though Shisui’s story is structurally homologous to her own arc, it is told with a stillness and seriousness that Maomao finds unfamiliar. Normally chatty and energetic, in this moment Shisui looks, to Maomao, like someone she has seen before but cannot place. She is reading Loulan without knowing it.

Kitamura’s analysis of Japanese women who shift between docile and assertive personas while carefully gauging the situation identifies a mode of agency that operates precisely through this kind of instability.36 Shisui’s dual identity is structurally identical to this logic. She does not resist the system of consort ranking and performance from outside it. She inhabits it completely, from inside, while simultaneously existing in a register the system cannot access. Wai-yee Li’s work on late Ming courtesan culture identifies a parallel dynamic: the most meaningful communications in constrained female social spaces occur not through direct speech but through the cultivation of shared interpretive margins.37

The ghost story Shisui tells exemplifies this by establishing the context of Loulan’s political situation as a layered metaphor. Loulan is expected to produce an heir and thereby fulfill the consort’s primary function. But her mother Shenmei’s plan goes further: Shenmei intends to parasitize Loulan’s imperial standing, to use the power Loulan accrues through childbirth as leverage for her own ambitions. Loulan is not only the woman who must produce the next generation through her body—she is herself being consumed by her mother’s schemes. With this context in mind, this story talks not only about the predatory feminine but also the predatory maternal, told by someone who experiences both.

In classical Chinese literary culture, the spider provides a structural model for the kind of feminized, lateral power Shisui exercises—though the series does not make this comparison explicitly. As a feminized figure in literary texts, the spider-woman represents the most extreme version of the predatory feminine: a woman whose seductiveness is not just performance but mechanism, whose web is the trap.3839 What this figure illuminates is a structural logic that The Apothecary Diaries extends into Shisui’s network-building. The web she builds out of the network of relationships across pavilions, the information systems that run through laundry routes and bathhouse sessions, the connections she teaches others to cultivate are architecturally spider-like: centered on a figure who appears to be at the margins, drawing connections inward, maintaining the structure through continuous, invisible labor. The women who pass through her network do not always know they are doing so. The web does the work the spider does not visibly perform.

The most extreme act in Shisui’s arc is the killing of her mother and her entire clan.

Matriphagy—the biological process by which offspring consume the maternal body as their first act of survival—is documented most extensively in certain spider species, where the mother’s body becomes the brood’s first meal, a final act of extraction that mirrors the logic of the relationship it ends. In the immediate aftermath of her mother’s death, Shisui dances on the wall—her mother’s blood still on her, not wiped away. The blood becomes a part of the dance, and the dance is not a performance of grief or guilt but of completion: the continuation of Loulan’s final role, the performance the court had already cast her in. The wall, in both Shisui’s and Maomao’s cases, is a liminal space: neither inside nor outside, neither past nor future, high enough that the gaze of the court cannot easily reach it. On the wall, both women briefly occupy a position the palace system cannot assign, regulate, or read.

Throughout Shisui’s arc, the series layers a second mythological register over the insect symbolism: the contrast between tanuki and kitsune, two shapeshifting figures of Japanese folklore whose differences matter precisely here. Shisui is the tanuki’s daughter—her father Shishou is coded in tanuki terms, a trickster whose shapeshifting is earthbound and reactive. Shisui inherits this quality: her dual identity is cunning and practical, organized around staying alive inside a system designed to use her. The kitsune—the nine-tailed fox—is a different figure. Tamamo-no-mae, the most famous kitsune in the Japanese literary tradition and the name that inspired Shisui’s new identity at the end, is a woman of extraordinary beauty and learning, who appeared at the imperial court of Emperor Konoe, capable of answering any question posed to her, and whose body produced its own light when the palace candles went dark.40 In Edo-period elaborations of the legend, the same spirit had previously appeared in China as Daji (妲己), the consort of the last Shang king, whose influence brought down an entire dynasty. This path from Chinese imperial court to Japanese imperial court is exactly the geography the fictional world of The Apothecary Diaries compresses its own hybrid setting. The tanuki shapeshifts to survive.

The kitsune shapeshifts to transcend. Shisui began as the former and metamorphosed into the latter.

Shisui’s final appearance resolves the insect symbolism that the series has been building.

She is in a coastal market, wearing her hair loose, trading the bullet-scarred hairpin—Maomao’s gift and the object that saved her life from the bullets—for a jade cicada at a vendor’s stall. The cicada Shisui chooses is not a burial object. She is not dead. She is renaming herself, becoming Tamamo, releasing Loulan, stepping out of the skin the court assigned her and the skin she built to survive it. The jade cicada is the statement of her completed metamorphosis.

Maomao’s method is organized around endurance within constraints. Shisui’s method is different. She does not endure the system. She sheds it. Where Maomao’s model is the apothecary—precise dosage, measured intervention, and careful management of knowledge—Shisui’s is the insect: transformation as the final form of survival, emergence as the escape that leaves nothing behind to be consumed. Together, the two methods map the full range of strategies The Apothecary Diaries proposes for women trapped inside masculine systems designed to consume them. Maomao ensures that as many people as possible survive the winter.

Shisui survives by becoming something that winter cannot reach.4142

5The Garden’s Essence

Characters, however, are not the whole story. The series makes deliberate choices that are not incidental to its arguments. They are the arguments. Understanding what those choices are, and what they inherit and recover, is the final puzzle piece that reveals the whole picture.

The mystery genre has a specific relationship to female pharmacological expertise.

Agatha Christie, the best-selling fiction writer in the Western tradition, is the most concentrated example. She trained as a hospital dispensary assistant during both World Wars, without formal pharmaceutical credentials, in exactly the kind of marginal, feminized wartime role that offered access without institutional standing.43 That access became the foundation of her entire career. She avoided guns in her stories because she knew nothing about ballistics. She used poisons because she knew everything about dosage. The Pharmaceutical Journal praised the accuracy of her first novel. In roughly half her stories, poison is the murder method. When Hyūga Natsu chose the mystery genre and built it around a woman whose pharmacological expertise is the narrative’s only reliable instrument, she was working within a tradition where that combination—female apothecary knowledge, no institutional authorization, detective logic—has produced the highest cultural authority the genre has ever generated. Christie did it as an author. The Apothecary Diaries does it at the level of character.

Genre matters not only for what it permits but for what it demands. Most series that enter the hanamachi or the imperial harem use these spaces as aesthetic backdrop: the pleasure quarter signals danger and beauty, the palace signals power and intrigue, and the women inside them are either tragic ornaments or femmes fatales. Demon Slayer: Kimetsu no Yaiba’s Entertainment District arc is a useful point of comparison. Its highest-ranking courtesan, the oiran Daki, turns out to be a demon. Her backstory gestures at exploitation and transformation, but that thread is absorbed almost immediately by the demands of shōnen action spectacle.44 The pleasure quarter functions as an atmosphere. The Apothecary Diaries refuses this narrative. Written by a female author and situated within the josei mystery framework, it presents both the hanamachi and the rear palace with fidelity to their mechanics and their costs.45 Josei mystery does not treat feminine-coded knowledge as incidental to the plot. It makes it the plot’s mechanism. In a shōnen, Maomao’s expertise would be a superpower deployed for action. In a josei mystery, it is the only instrument through which the narrative moves at all.

The structure of the series deepens this alignment. The Apothecary Diaries is not a pure mystery or a pure court drama. It operates through a deliberate hybrid: mystery supplies the episodic investigative structure that makes Maomao’s expertise the engine of each narrative unit, the court drama supplies the political stakes that make her caution necessary., and the diary tradition supplies the observational interiority that gives her knowledge its authority without requiring institutional validation. In a pure court drama, Maomao’s pharmacological competence would be background detail, subordinated to political intrigue. In a pure mystery without the court structure, the stakes would be detachable from her specific position. The hybrid is what forces the three registers into alignment: the thing all three genres need from each other is a woman who can read what the court cannot.

What the series recovers, and transmits, is a tradition. The figures that Maomao and Shisui embody are not invented. These figures have real histories behind them—Tan Yunxian compiling the Nǜyī zayan without institutional sanction, the Lady Who Loved Insects refusing conventional femininity a thousand years before Shisui did it in an imperial consort’s robes, Tamamo-no-mae illuminating a darkened palace with her own body before anyone was able to “read” what she really was. The series distills these figures: extracts what is essential and carries it forward in a form that reaches readers who will never encounter a Ming-dynasty medical casebook or a Heian court memoir. More than twenty-two million manga copies sold means more than twenty-two million encounters with this tradition, however briefly and however mediated.46 That is what a garden produces when it is also something else: not just flowers for display, but essence: something concentrated, portable, and capable of traveling far beyond its walls.

The survival strategies the series recovers are not safely historical. The pattern both Maomao and Shisui navigate—female competence treated as institutionally inconvenient, formal authority reserved for men, knowledge that persists because it was useful rather than because it was permitted—has not disappeared in the world that receives the series. As mentioned earlier, in 2018, the year the anime’s source novels were gaining global traction, it emerged that multiple Japanese medical schools had for years systematically reduced women’s entrance examination scores to limit female enrollment. The mechanism differs from Maomao’s palace, but the assumption does not. When audiences encounter a woman who knows exactly what the institution will not let her say she knows, they are not encountering a historical artifact, but a continuous legacy of the garden systems.

Conclusion

The Apothecary Diaries explores women navigating and subtly subverting oppressive systems, finding autonomy in the very tools those systems extend and withhold. Female agency in this series does not appear as rebellion. For Maomao, it is endurance, calibration, and the quiet persistence of knowledge the court refuses to authorize. For Shisui, it is something more radical: not endurance but metamorphosis, the shedding of her name and constraints the system imposes.47 Together, they carve out different shapes of the same refusal—Maomao saves lives that would have been lost to ignorance or malice, brings a measure of justice where there would have been none, and navigates class divides without ever seeking rank. Shisui walks out of the court as something the court had no category for.

In reimagining literary archetypes, The Apothecary Diaries redefines what a female protagonist can be. She does not need to fit the mold of a warrior or a romantic lead. She can be plain, she can be odd, she can care more about toxins than marriage, and her story can still grip millions. The popularity of the novels, manga, and anime suggests that readers are ready for exactly this. As one critic puts it, the series is “an incomparable remedy for tired tropes.”48 It offers something at once new and, in its bones, familiar: palace intrigue told with the voice of a woman who, acting like an herb in the shade, does the work no one is watching for.

Maomao’s method is an argument about what female strength can look like. She does not overthrow the systems that shaped her, nor does she transcend them. She navigates them with precision, and in doing so, she ensures that some of the people those systems would discard survive. That is not a small thing. It is a form of power that is quiet, calibrated, and genuinely difficult to maintain.49 Shisui’s method does not calibrate her presence within the garden: she exits it, becoming something the garden’s taxonomy cannot hold. One method sustains those who must stay inside the walls. The other belongs to those who find their way around it.

Together, they account for what the garden actually produces: not only the flowers it intended. The Apothecary Diaries does not limit female agency to the choice between Maomao and Shisui.

Both are legitimate, and both are different expressions of the same refusal to wither. The lady who loved insects knew that the prettiest butterflies are but the sheddings of caterpillars, and that the ornamental result is never the whole story.

Gardens take many forms. Courtesans trained to perform desire on command.

Concubines ranked by children not yet born. Daughters delivered to foreign courts as part of arrangements no one asked them to ratify. Women photographed, measured, and told when their season had passed. The walls change shape. The seasons change names. But in every incarnation, it is still the same garden: flowers cultivated for display, insects that sustain the bloom and devour it from within, and everything in between that grew without being planted. The thorns. An herb. Wildflowers. A woman turned so poisonous that she was called a monster. A birth that devoured the mother. A web in the corners. Shells shed in emergence. The hum in the walls. The glow in the dark. The scent no wall could hold. All of it is the garden. The garden was never only its flowers.
